# AI writing: forbidden patterns

## What this is

Patterns that read as AI-tells in formal writing. A curated list, built up across many cleanup passes on real academic prose. This list covers what to avoid; pair it with a positive structure guide of your choice for what to do.

The list is organized A through T, with sub-rules under each category. The Standing rules at the bottom are meta-principles for catching new instances that don't appear on the explicit list yet.

One caveat when applying this to personal writing (email especially): the test is sincerity, not blandness. Genuine warmth, hedging, and effusiveness are voice, not tells. Cut phrasing that sounds generic or performative; keep phrasing that sounds like the writer, even when it's soft.

## Use when

- **Before drafting:** skim to remind yourself which moves to avoid.
- **After drafting:** run a search-and-replace audit on the prose against the patterns below.
- **When reviewing AI-generated drafts:** these are the highest-yield things to strip first.

## A. Punctuation and formatting

- Em-dashes (—): replace with commas, parentheses, semicolons, or split into separate sentences
- Inline italics (`*word*` mid-sentence for emphasis): structural italics for citations or proper terminology only
- Inline bold mid-sentence (`**word**` for emphasis, not for bullet labels or headers)
- Hyphenated emphasis as em-dash substitute
- Bold-then-italic combinations
- Single-word sentences for emphasis ("Wrong.")
- Trailing dramatic ellipsis

## B. Discourse markers

Sentence-leading or set-off connectives that telegraph rhetorical moves rather than do work. Drop or restructure:

- *In fact, Indeed, Of course, Naturally, Clearly, Obviously*
- *Crucially, Notably, Importantly, Critically, Strikingly, Tellingly, Significantly, Surprisingly, Interestingly, Remarkably*
- *Moreover, Furthermore, Additionally, Hence, Thus, Therefore*
- *By contrast, In contrast, Conversely, Similarly, Likewise*
- *On this reading, Read together, Read in light of, Concretely:*
- *In essence, In summary, In conclusion, Practically speaking, In short, Briefly*
- *That said, To be clear, In other words, Put simply, Put differently*
- *It turns out, The fact is, Having established X, Building on X, With X in hand*

## C. Strong declarative pivots

- "X is not Y. It is Z." (period or semicolon variants)
- "X is not a Y but a Z."
- "X are not Y. They are Z."
- "Not X. Y." (aphoristic pairs)
- "It is X that Y" clefts
- "X is one thing; Y is quite another"

## D. Cleft sentences

- "What X is is Y"
- "What gets X is Y"
- "What this means is Y"
- "What this looks like in practice is..."
- "It is X that Y"

## E. Punchline interjections

Set-off phrases that act as rhetorical buttons. Strip them or restructure:

- *, together, / , collectively, / , in turn, / , in tandem, / , taken together, / , in concert, / , in combination,*
- *, on balance, / , by and large, / , for the most part, / , broadly speaking,*
- *, to some extent, / , in some sense, / , in a way,*
- *, which is to say, / , that is to say, / , or rather,*

## F. Semicolon-paired aphorisms

- Setup-then-pivot rhythm joined by semicolon with a short contrastive second clause
- "X serves Y; none is Z"
- "X does A; B does not"

## G. Grandiose metaphors

- *the methodological keystone, the operative claim, the central thrust, lies at the heart of, comes down to, the crux of*
- *speaks to, cuts against, comports with, stands in tension with, makes contact with*
- *sits at the intersection of, sits in, sits at, sits with, sits between*
- *lives in, occupies (a position/territory)*
- *the essence of, the bedrock, the cornerstone*
- *unlocking, uncovering*
- *tells a story, the story is, tells us, a clear story*
- *sheds light on, casts light on, in a different light*
- *through this/that/a/the lens, framing, stance, register, posture* (as substitutes for plain "view" or "approach")
- *What we see is..., What emerges is...*
- *advances a [reading/claim/account/argument]*
- *the natural reading*
- *sits in the family of*

## H. Recursive flourishes

- "is itself a contribution"
- "is itself part of"
- "in itself" / "in and of itself"
- "do this work"
- "the rest of the investigation depends on"
- "what the thesis offers/provides/contributes/delivers"

## I. Author-as-character (rhetorical)

- "I treat X as" (when rhetorical, not substantive)
- "I view X as"
- "I take X to be"
- "I read X as"
- "I would argue / contend / maintain / claim"
- "It is my view"
- "My position is"

## J. Hyperbolic intensifiers

- *actually, really, genuinely, essentially, effectively*
- *fundamentally, ultimately, inherently*
- *robust(ly)* (when not technical)
- *comprehensive(ly), non-trivial(ly)*
- *remarkably, strikingly, meaningfully, substantively*
- *deeply, profoundly, thoroughly*
- *compelling, powerful, elegant, sophisticated, rich*
- *principled, rigorous, extensive* (when self-applied)
- *very, quite, rather* (as intensifiers)
- *particularly, especially* (when overused)

## K. Punchy closers

Short impact-only sentences whose only purpose is rhetorical:

- "X reflects Y, not Z" sting closers
- "The upshot is..., The takeaway is..., The point is..."
- "All of which is to say..., Which brings us to..."
- "X, full stop." / "X, period."

## L. Meta-claims about the work's value

The most common AI register issue. Drop these in favor of substantive content.

### Comparative self-positioning

- "is what sets this work apart"
- "is what distinguishes"
- "this reframing"
- "this stance"
- "is positioned as"

### Self-strengthening verbs

- "support the contribution"
- "strengthen the contribution"
- "sharpen the result"
- "weaken/undermine the contribution"
- "is reflected in"

### Grand-claim closers

- "has consequences for"
- "has implications for"
- "reshapes how X should be read"
- "bears on the broader literature"

### Self-evaluating stance verbs

- "the paper approaches X as"
- "the paper treats X as"
- "the paper is committed to"

### Self-defending parallels

"X, not Y" or "X rather than Y" used to label the work's own choices as principled:

- "X is a deliberate scope boundary rather than a flaw"
- "X is a feature, not a bug"
- "X is by design, not by accident"
- "X is intentional rather than [negative-thing]"
- "X is principled rather than [negative-thing]"

### Self-aggrandizing nouns

- "the novelty / innovation of the work"
- "what makes the work distinctive / special / unique"
- "the paper aims to / seeks to / sets out to" (when grandiose)
- "the paper stakes a claim on"
- "the paper contributes [N] [evaluative-adjective] [structural noun]"

### Evaluative descriptors paired with structural nouns

- *tightly coupled, well-integrated, carefully connected*
- *cleanly separated, neatly factored*
- *rigorous, principled, comprehensive, systematic* (when self-praise rather than description)

### Abstract-subject + formal-verb topic sentences that announce rather than do

- "The [design / factorization / formulation / approach / setup / configuration / framework] permits / allows / enables / provides / yields / affords / supports / delivers [vague evaluative object]"
- "The [paper / investigation / work / study] offers / provides / yields / delivers [abstract noun]"
- "This [approach / setup / formulation] gives us [Y]"
- "Such an approach permits Y"

### Vague evaluative objects to avoid pairing with these verbs

- "clean answers" / "clear answers"
- "questions of interest" / "cases of interest" / "features of interest" / "variables of interest"
- "a principled X" / "a sharp picture"

### Evaluative-judgment bridges between contrasting positions

- "X may be premature / incomplete / mistaken / misleading / questionable / wrong"
- "X is too quick / too facile / too neat"
- "X warrants / merits / deserves reconsideration"
- "X is open to question / open to challenge"

### Empty structural meta-promises

Sentences that announce how a list or section will be addressed without doing the addressing. The pattern: list-item subject (or vague structural subject) + modal of requirement + vague abstract noun.

- "Each requires a distinct account / treatment / analysis / discussion / explanation"
- "Each demands separate consideration / attention / examination"
- "Each warrants its own treatment / analysis"
- "Each calls for individual analysis"
- "We address each in turn" (when "in turn" adds no information)
- "Each is addressed below"
- "These questions deserve careful treatment"
- "Each of these points merits discussion"

The hallmarks: vague abstract noun ("account", "treatment", "perspective", "discussion") that names no actual content; modal of requirement ("requires", "demands", "warrants", "deserves", "calls for"); self-administered roadmap announcing structure to come instead of delivering it. The structure of the prose is already implicit in the section flow; the meta-promise adds zero information. Drop it; start the substantive discussion directly. Closely related to the "Abstract-subject + formal-verb topic sentences" pattern above, but here the subject is a list-pronoun ("each") rather than a framework noun.

## M. Padding

- "It is important to note, It is worth noting, It bears mentioning, It should be noted"
- *in order to* (vs *to*), *due to the fact that* (vs *because*), *in spite of the fact that* (vs *though*)
- *with respect to, in respect of, as regards, with regard to*
- *in the context of, by virtue of, for the purposes of*
- "There exists X" (vs "X exists")
- "It can be shown that"

## N. Triadic and parallel overuse

- "X, Y, and Z" appearing more than three times per paragraph
- Triadic adjective stacks
- "not only X but also Y"
- "not just X but also Y"
- "neither X nor Y" (when rhetorical)

## O. Hedge stacking

- "It seems that X may potentially..."
- "It could be argued that X might possibly..."
- "appears to suggest"
- "seems to suggest"
- "might possibly" / "may potentially" / "could possibly"

## P. Performative humility

- "This is, of course, a simplification"
- "It would be wrong to suggest"
- "It would be a mistake"
- "One might object that"
- "A skeptic might say"
- "To be sure, Granted, Admittedly"

## Q. Pseudo-academic phrasings

- *advances a different reading, the natural reading, tells a clear story, sits in the family*
- "Studying X this way calls for Y"
- "The factorization permits clean answers"
- "The paper offers a mechanistic answer"
- "In the [conference-name] X sense"
- *If borne out, if X obtains, bear out / borne out*
- *calls for* (when *requires* or *needs* works)
- *permits clean / clean answers*

Stilted verbs in non-technical contexts (replace with plain English):

- "does not yield" / "yields" (when not in mathematical/statistical/technical context, replace with "produces," "results in," or rewrite)
- "begets / engenders / occasions / elicits"
- "affords" (when "allows" or "gives" works)

Metaphorical drivers (replace with literal language):

- "X drives the timeline / drives the schedule"
- "Two hard deadlines drive..."

## R. Impersonal-academic pronouns

- "one might / one would / one could / one may"
- "one expects / one assumes / one thinks / one imagines / one takes / one sees / one notes / one observes"
- "as one might / as one would"
- "the reader might / the reader could / the reader will see"
- "we might / we would" (used impersonally, where "we" = "you, the reader")
- "it might be expected / it would seem / it might appear"

## S. Back-reference shorthand

- "the former" / "the latter" (when the reader has to look back to figure out which)
- "the first" / "the second" referring to abstract nouns rather than to numbered list items

## T. Archaic and overly formal hedges

- *if borne out, borne out, bear out*
- *if X obtains, where X obtains*
- *ought to* (when *should* works)
- *shall* (when *will* works)
- *stands to reason*
- *to that end / to this end*
- *in any event / at any rate / in any case* (as connectors)
- *in keeping with, in accordance with*
- *owing to, by way of, by dint of*
- *insofar as, inasmuch as*
- *in so doing, in doing so*
- *whereupon, wherein, whereby, thereby, therein*
- *perforce, ipso facto, prima facie, ex hypothesi*
- *qua, à la, viz., to wit*

## Standing rules (meta-principles)

These are the heuristics for catching patterns that aren't on the explicit list yet:

- **Bridging:** when tempted to bridge with a discourse marker, restructure the sentences instead.
- **Self-evaluation:** when tempted to evaluate the contribution, describe what the work does and let the reader evaluate.
- **Aphorism:** when tempted to use a short, aphoristic sentence for impact, ask whether the substance carries weight without rhythmic flourish.
- **Pseudo-academic:** when tempted to pair an inflated verb with an abstract noun ("the factorization permits," "the paper contributes"), just say the thing plainly.
- **Stilted register:** if a phrase reads like a 19th-century court opinion or Victorian essay, replace it with the plainest modern equivalent.
- **Back-references:** name the thing directly. Impersonal pronouns and back-references push cognitive work onto the reader that the writer can do for them.
- **Evaluative bridges:** when bridging between two contrasting positions, identify the implicit assumption being challenged or the specific gap being filled. Don't grade the inference.
- **Topic sentences:** when tempted to write a topic sentence that announces what the next sentence is going to say, just write the next sentence.
- **Defensive parallels:** state the design choice and let the reader judge whether it's deliberate. Pre-empting an objection plants the suggestion that there might be one.
- **Technical vs evaluative:** the same word can be precise in one register and pretentious in another. *Yield* in stats/math is fine ("the resamples yield the interval"); *yield* in evaluative contexts is stilted ("the cycle yields a decision"). Context determines register.
